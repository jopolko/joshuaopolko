CHILD ABUSE IN RELIGIOUS COMMUNITIES: WHAT THE RESEARCH SHOWS

An evidence review of what elevates, and what reduces, the risk of child abuse inside religious communities.

Religion by itself does not cause child abuse. Large population studies consistently find that ordinary religious participation is, on balance, more protective than harmful: it correlates with stronger social networks, lower rates of some forms of family violence, and more adults invested in a child's wellbeing. The finding that actually matters is narrower and more uncomfortable. A recognizable set of structural features, not beliefs or denominations, predicts where abuse concentrates and where it stays hidden. Authoritarian control, insularity, the sacralizing of leaders, an instinct to protect the institution before the child, corporal-punishment theology, and legal reporting exemptions are the variables. A community carrying several of them is measurably more dangerous for children than one that carries none, whatever its theology. This is what the public inquiries and the peer-reviewed research show when you read them side by side.

The scale the inquiries documented

Two of the largest investigations ever conducted into institutional abuse set the baseline. In the United States, the John Jay College study commissioned by the US Conference of Catholic Bishops examined 1950 to 2002 and documented 10,667 individuals who alleged sexual abuse as minors, with records sufficient to identify 4,392 accused clergy, roughly 4 percent of all priests active in that period [1]. The survey covered 195 dioceses, about 98 percent of diocesan priests, so it is not a sample but close to a census.

In Australia, the Royal Commission into Institutional Responses to Child Sexual Abuse heard from nearly 6,900 survivors in private sessions between 2013 and 2017. 58.6 percent reported abuse in a religious institution, and of those, 61.8 percent described Catholic settings and 14.7 percent Anglican ones [2]. The Commission issued 409 recommendations and concluded that religious organisations were significantly overrepresented among the institutions where children were harmed [2].

These figures are not an indictment of faith. They are an indictment of how specific institutions were structured and governed, and the same inquiries that counted the victims also diagnosed the mechanism. The point of reviewing them is not to condemn belief but to identify the conditions that let harm flourish, because those conditions are fixable.

Religion is not the variable. Structure is.

It is tempting to read the numbers as "religion is dangerous." The research does not support that flat conclusion, and saying so is not a defense of negligent institutions. It is a sharper accusation. A German government-sponsored reappraisal study comparing abuse in religiously affiliated and secular institutions found that the institutional dynamics, exploitation of hierarchy, dependence, and trust, were the common thread, not the presence of religion as such [5]. A 2024 analysis of abuse perpetrated by leaders and other adults in religious organizations in Australia reached a similar place: risk tracked with closed systems, power asymmetry, and proximity, the conditions that let a trusted adult offend repeatedly without detection [6].

What the literature does isolate is that certain orientations raise risk. An older risk-factor assessment found that victims abused by a relative were disproportionately affiliated with fundamentalist Protestant households, a signal about family structure and authority rather than about scripture [8]. The scholarship on "religious authority as a risk factor" makes the mechanism explicit: when a leader is treated as a conduit for the divine, the ordinary social checks that would flag predatory behavior are suspended, because challenging the leader feels like challenging God [4]. Religion is the setting. Authority structure is the cause.

Risk factor one: authoritarian, high-control authority

The single most consistent finding across the inquiries is that abuse concentrates where authority is absolute and unaccountable. When a pastor, elder, or priest holds spiritual power, social prestige, and practical control over members' lives at once, three things happen. Offenders are drawn to the access and cover the role provides. Victims and their families are conditioned to defer rather than question. And the surrounding community reads any allegation as an attack on the institution itself [4]. The Royal Commission found that the cultures most associated with abuse shared patriarchal and authoritarian beliefs, weak oversight, and poor screening of the people given access to children [2].

High-control environments also normalize obedience as the supreme virtue. A child taught that questioning an adult is a sin is a child stripped of the instinct that protects others. The same deference that the community frames as righteousness is, in practice, the grooming work already done.

Risk factor two: insularity and the closed system

Risk rises further in closed systems. Research on institutional abuse repeatedly finds that insularity, distrust of outside authority, and discouragement of contact with the secular world generate a pattern in which power and proximity quietly facilitate abuse and suppress its discovery [6]. A community that handles its own problems internally, that treats police, social workers, and courts as hostile or unspiritual, removes exactly the external eyes that catch what insiders are trained not to see.

Insularity compounds with control. The more a group isolates children from mandated reporters, secular schooling, and outside friendships, the fewer independent adults exist who could notice an injury, hear a disclosure, or make a report. Closure is not incidental to the harm. It is the environment the harm requires.

Risk factor three: protecting the institution before the child

When abuse is discovered, the decisive variable is what the institution does next, and here the inquiries are damning. The Royal Commission catalogued a recurring pattern of institutional response: denial and minimisation, failure to report alleged perpetrators to civil authorities, transferring offenders between parishes or locations, and acting to limit scandal and legal liability rather than to protect children [2]. The reflex to preserve the institution's reputation routinely overrode the duty to protect the child.

The clearest single illustration came from the Royal Commission's case study of the Jehovah's Witnesses. The organisation's Australian records held 1,006 alleged perpetrators reported to elders since 1950, relating to more than 1,800 victims, including 579 cases in which the perpetrator confessed. Not one had been reported to police by the organisation [3]. A central cause was the group's "two-witness rule," a requirement of two eyewitnesses to establish wrongdoing in the absence of confession, a standard the Commission said reflected a serious failure to understand how child sexual abuse actually occurs, almost always without witnesses [3]. The rule is not unique to one denomination. It is a template: an internal evidentiary bar that makes confirmed abuse nearly impossible to act on, set by the same body that has every incentive not to act.

Risk factor four: corporal punishment as doctrine

A separate strand of harm comes not from predators but from theology that prescribes pain. Where a community reads "spare the rod and spoil the child" as a literal parenting mandate, physical punishment is reframed as righteousness, and the line between discipline and abuse blurs by design. Research on this orientation found that a rigid, conformity-driven religious outlook was associated with higher child-abuse potential, precisely because deviation from authority is treated as intolerable and punishment as a duty [7].

The most documented example is the self-published manual To Train Up a Child by Michael and Debi Pearl, which advocates striking children, including infants, with implements such as plastic tubing and switches, and frames abandoning the practice as abandoning belief in God and eternity [9]. The book's methods have been linked to the deaths of at least three children whose parents followed its teachings: Sean Paddock, seven-year-old Lydia Schatz, who was whipped to death for mispronouncing a word, and 11-year-old Hana Williams, who died of hypothermia and malnutrition after prolonged punishment [9]. The prosecutor in the Schatz case called it "an extraordinarily dangerous book for those who take it literally" [9]. The doctrine matters because it converts the parents themselves, not an outside predator, into the source of harm, and wraps that harm in religious obligation.

The legal shield: reporting exemptions

The structural risks above are reinforced by law. In the United States, 33 states exempt clergy from mandatory child-abuse reporting when the information is learned through a communication the church deems privileged, the clergy-penitent privilege [10]. In practice, an offender can confess abuse to a religious leader and that leader may have no legal duty, and under church policy sometimes an affirmative duty not, to tell anyone. Reporting on the loophole found that over two decades more than 130 bills sought to create or amend these reporting laws, and that efforts to close the clergy exemption repeatedly failed amid organised opposition from religious institutions, including the Catholic Church, the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, and the Jehovah's Witnesses [10]. A handful of jurisdictions, among them New Hampshire and West Virginia, deny the privilege in child-abuse cases [10], which shows the exemption is a policy choice, not an inevitability.

What actually protects children

The same research that maps the risk factors implies the remedy, and the remedy is structural, not theological. The protective conditions are the mirror image of the dangerous ones:

  • External accountability. Independent oversight of anyone with access to children, real screening and training, and a governance structure in which no leader is beyond question [2].
  • Mandatory reporting with no clergy carve-out. A legal duty to report suspected abuse to civil authorities, with the clergy-penitent exemption closed, so that disclosure reaches police rather than dying inside the institution [10].
  • Breaking insularity. Contact with mandated reporters, secular schooling, and the outside world, so independent adults exist who can notice an injury and act on it [6].
  • Believing and acting on disclosures. Removing the alleged offender rather than transferring them, and treating a child's account as evidence rather than scandal [2].
  • Retiring internal evidentiary bars. No two-witness rule or equivalent standard that makes real abuse impossible to confirm and convenient to ignore [3].

None of this requires a community to abandon its faith. It requires it to surrender the specific structures, unaccountable authority, closure, and self-protection, that the evidence ties to harm. The communities where children are safest are not the least religious. They are the ones most willing to be watched.
