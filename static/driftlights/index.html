Driftlights: WebXR Depth of Field and the Spatial Judgment Behind Architectural VR

Most "WebXR demos" are a clever shader painted on the inside of a sphere. They look great until you move, and then you notice the whole universe is glued to your face, there's no there there. Driftlights is the opposite. It's a real volume of hundreds of glowing orbs hanging at fixed points in space, rendered with genuine optical depth of field, so the near ones melt into soft bokeh and the far ones haze into fog. Orbit around and they stay put. Click, and you leave a light behind that's still there when you come back. Put on a headset and you can walk among them.

That last part is the whole reason this demo matters beyond the eye candy. The depth-of-field bokeh, the parallax as orbs slide past each other, and the way every light stays anchored where you placed it are not decoration: they are the depth cues your visual system uses to size up a real space. In a headset your two eyes give you true binocular depth, which is exactly what lets you judge clearances, sightlines, ceiling height and proportion at 1:1, and catch problems a flat plan or a pretty render quietly hides. That is the spatial judgment that architectural VR depends on, and Driftlights is a stripped-down way to feel it working.

The point of the demo: two things the shader demos can't do, depth of field (a real focal plane with bokeh, not a flat filter) and object permanence (objects anchored in world space that persist as you move and as you place new ones).

Key takeaways

  • Genuine depth of field: A BokehPass reads the scene's depth buffer to blur by distance from the focal plane, giving real bokeh rather than a flat screen-space filter.
  • Object permanence: Every orb sits at a fixed world coordinate and stays there as you orbit, giving the scene a structure your eye can trust.
  • True parallax: Near and far orbs slide past each other correctly as the camera moves, the same spatial cue your visual system uses to judge distance in a real room.
  • Architectural connection: These depth cues are exactly what let architects judge clearances, sightlines, and ceiling height at 1:1 scale inside a VR headset.
  • WebXR ready: Press Enter VR on any WebXR-capable headset and step inside the field; your own two eyes supply real binocular depth, replacing the on-screen bokeh simulation.
  • Interactive controls: Adjust focus distance, aperture, bloom strength, and drift speed in real time with the on-screen sliders.

What are you actually looking at?

The blur is the whole trick. Real cameras have a focal plane: things at one distance are sharp, and everything nearer or farther falls out of focus into round highlights, bokeh. Driftlights renders that honestly using the scene's depth buffer, not a fake vignette. Slide Focus and watch the sharp band travel through the field; slide Aperture to open the lens wider and drown the edges in creamy circles.

The orbs are not a backdrop. Each one is a real mesh at a fixed coordinate, which is what makes object permanence obvious: as the camera drifts, near and far orbs slide past each other with true parallax, the way real objects do. Click anywhere and you drop a new light into the world at that point. Orbit away, come back, it's still glowing exactly where you left it, casting light on its neighbours. Those exact cues, things holding their place in space and shifting with parallax as you move, are what let an architect read a room at true scale: a column that sits closer than the drawing implied, a beam that drops lower than you pictured, a corridor that feels tighter once you can actually move past it.

How does Driftlights work?

It's Three.js with a small post-processing stack. A normal render pass draws the orbs, then a BokehPass reads the depth buffer and blurs by distance from the focal plane, an UnrealBloomPass adds the glow, and an OutputPass tone-maps it. The lights are MeshStandardMaterial orbs with emissive color; the ones you place carry a real PointLight so they illuminate the field around them.

WebXR needs to render straight to the headset's framebuffer per eye, which screen-space post-processing fights with. So the loop branches: on desktop it runs the full bokeh-and-bloom composer; the moment you Enter VR it switches to a direct stereo render. You lose the artificial bokeh in the headset, but you gain the real thing, because now your own two eyes are doing the depth. That swap is the point: the on-screen blur is a convincing imitation, but true depth lives in the stereo pair, which is why a 1:1 walkthrough lets you judge a space in ways a monitor never quite can.

What should you try in Driftlights?

The sliders and click interaction each reveal a different aspect of the optical rendering. These four experiments isolate one variable at a time so the underlying mechanism becomes obvious.

  • Rack focus. Drag Focus slowly from 4 to 22 and watch the sharp plane sweep front to back.
  • Open the lens. Push Aperture up for dreamy, almost abstract bokeh; pull it to zero for everything-in-focus.
  • Leave a trail. Click around the volume to plant your own lights, then orbit to see them hold position.
  • Go in. On a Quest or any WebXR headset, hit Enter VR and use the trigger to place lights at your controller.

Frequently asked questions

What is depth of field in WebGL?

Depth of field is the optical effect where only objects at a chosen distance (the focal plane) are sharp while nearer and farther objects blur into bokeh. In WebGL it's done as a post-processing pass that samples the scene's depth buffer and blurs each pixel by how far it sits from the focal distance.

What does "object permanence" mean in a 3D demo?

It means the objects live at fixed world coordinates rather than being painted onto your view. They persist as you move, show true parallax, and stay where you place them. It's the difference between a real 3D scene and a screen-space shader that follows the camera.

What does this have to do with architectural VR?

The same depth cues that make these orbs read as a real volume, true bokeh, binocular depth and parallax, and object permanence, are what let you judge a building before it exists. In an immersive headset your two eyes give genuine depth at 1:1 scale, so you can size up clearances, sightlines, ceiling height and proportion directly and catch clashes a flat plan or a flattering render hides. That is the core promise of architectural visualization in VR: design-stage problem detection by spatial judgment rather than by reading a drawing. Driftlights is a minimal sandbox for the perception that makes it work.

Why does immersive depth beat 2D plans and renders?

A plan or a single render flattens everything to one viewpoint, so distance, scale and proportion are inferred rather than felt. Stereo depth and parallax let you perceive how far apart things actually are and walk around them, which is how the eye natively measures a space. That is why a problem like a too-low soffit or a pinched walkway often only becomes obvious at true scale in a headset, not on the page.

Do I need a VR headset to use it?

No. On desktop or mobile you orbit by dragging and place lights by clicking, with full bokeh depth of field. A WebXR headset is optional, it just lets you step inside the field and place lights with a controller, where your own two eyes supply the true depth. No app install, no downloads.
